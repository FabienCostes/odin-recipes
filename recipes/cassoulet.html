Cassoulet recipe !


Cassoulet, a hearty and comforting dish, originates from the Languedoc region of southern France, with Toulouse, Castelnaudary, and Carcassonne claiming variations of the recipe.
Named after the cassole, the earthenware pot in which it’s traditionally cooked, cassoulet was initially a humble peasant meal designed to stretch simple ingredients. Over time, it gained recognition as a symbol of French culinary tradition, celebrated for its slow-cooked, richly flavored combination of white beans, duck or goose confit, pork, and sausage.
Its depth of flavor and satisfying nature make it a timeless favorite, particularly in colder months.

Ingredients (serves 6-8):

  • 500 g dried white beans (e.g., Tarbais or cannellini)
  • 2 duck legs (confit, if possible)
  • 200 g pork belly or salted pork
  • 2 Toulouse sausages
  • 1 onion, finely chopped
  • 2 carrots, diced
  • 4 garlic cloves, minced
  • 2 tomatoes, peeled and chopped (or 1/2 cup canned tomatoes)
  • 1 bouquet garni (thyme, bay leaf, and parsley tied together)
  • 1 liter chicken or vegetable stock
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • Breadcrumbs for topping (optional)

Instructions :

  1. Prepare the beans: Soak them overnight in cold water. Drain, rinse, and cook in fresh water for about 1 hour until tender but firm. Drain and set aside.
  2. Sear the meats: In a large ovenproof pot, brown the duck legs, pork belly, and sausages. Remove and set aside.
  3. Sauté vegetables: In the same pot, cook the onions, carrots, and garlic until softened. Add the tomatoes and cook for a few minutes.
  4. Combine ingredients: Add the beans to the pot with the bouquet garni and stock. Season with salt and pepper. Nestle the meats into the beans.
  5. Simmer: Cover and cook gently for about 2 hours, stirring occasionally. Add water if needed to keep the beans covered.
  6. Bake (optional): Preheat your oven to 180°C (350°F). Sprinkle breadcrumbs over the cassoulet and bake uncovered for about 30 minutes to create a golden crust.
  7. Serve: Remove the bouquet garni and serve hot with crusty bread

The cassoulet is more than a dish—it’s a celebration of tradition, community, and the rich culinary heritage of Toulouse.
